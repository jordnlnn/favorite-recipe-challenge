Navajo Frybread

A Tradition of Resilience & Flavor

Golden, crispy, and deeply rooted in Native American culture, frybread is more than just food — it's a symbol of history, survival, and connection.

More Than Just Food

Frybread is more than a simple dish—it's a testament to the resilience, strength, and spirit of the Navajo people. Born out of hardship during the Long Walk of 1864, when families were forcibly removed from their lands and given only meager rations, frybread became a symbol of survival.

With just flour, salt, water, and ingenuity, they transformed scarcity into sustenance, creating a food that would carry the stories of generations. Today, frybread remains a cherished tradition, served at family gatherings, powwows, and celebrations, connecting the past with the present.

Let's Get Cooking!

What You'll Need:

  • 4 cups all-purpose flour
  • 1 1/2 tsp salt
  • 1 tbsp baking powder
  • 1 1/2 cups warm water
  • 1-2 cups vegetable oil (for frying)
  • Extra flour for dusting

The Cooking Process

  • In a large bowl, combine flour, salt, and baking powder. Slowly add warm water and mix until a soft dough forms.

  • Cover the dough and let it rest for 30 minutes. This helps it soften and develop texture.

  • Divide the dough into equal portions. Flatten each into a round disc, about ¼-inch thick.

  • Heat 1-2 inches of oil in a deep pan to 350°F (175°C). Test by dropping a small piece of dough—if it sizzles, it's ready.

  • Carefully place a dough disc into the hot oil. Fry for 1-2 minutes per side, flipping once, until golden brown. Drain on paper towels.

  • Eat warm with honey, powdered sugar, or as the base for Navajo tacos.

Toppings, Twists & Traditions

Plain

Sometimes, simple is best! Freshly fried and golden brown, plain frybread is delicious on its own.

Sweet

A treat for the tast buds! Drizzle with honey, dust with powdered sugar, or top with strawberries and Nutella.

Savory

Turn frybread into a hearty meal! Top it with seasoned meat, beans, lettuce, tomatoes, and cheese to make a classic Navajo taco.
